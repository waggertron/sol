## Summary

Since 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel have been at war with Iran and its regional allies. Hostilities broke out after US–Israeli airstrikes targeting Iranian military and government sites resulted in the assassination of Iranian officials and supreme leader Ali Khamenei. These attacks were launched amidst Iran–US negotiations to reach a nuclear peace agreement. Iran fired back missiles and drones on Israel, US-aligned Arab countries, and US bases; the United Arab Emirates, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait retaliated. Iran blocked the Strait of Hormuz, which disrupted the global economy by causing a fuel crisis.
In January, the Iran government massacred thousands of civilians in a crackdown on the country's largest protests since the Iranian Revolution in 1979. President Donald Trump responded by starting the largest US military buildup in the region since the 2003 invasion of Iraq.
In Iran, US–Israeli airstrikes damaged military bases, government buildings, schools, hospitals, and heritage sites, while also inflicting civilian casualties. In retaliation, the Iranian military launched hundreds of drones and ballistic missiles at Israel and Arab countries, including Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Oman, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates, as well as the Kurdistan Region of Iraq (KRI). At the same time, the Hezbollah–Israel conflict escalated into the 2026 Lebanon war.
The Trump administration gave various explanations for starting the war, including forestalling Iranian retaliation after an expected Israeli attack, destroying Iran's missile capabilities, preventing Iran from building a nuclear weapon, seizing Iran's oil  and gas resources, or regime change. Iranian and some US officials rejected claims that Iran had been preparing an attack. The International Atomic Energy Agency said that Iran had an "ambitious nuclear program" but there was no evidence that it had a "structured program to manufacture nuclear weapons." United Nations secretary-general António Guterres and several countries condemned the US–Israeli strikes; the UN Security Council passed a resolution condemning Iran's retaliatory strikes on Gulf states. Critics, including legal and international relations experts, described the US attacks as illegal under US law and a violation of Iran's sovereignty.
The war's economic impact includes the largest ever supply disruption of the global oil market, disruptions to the natural gas, fertilizer, aviation and tourism industries, as well as volatility in financial markets. Oil and gas shipments were disrupted by Iran's closure of the Strait of Hormuz, and Israeli and Iranian attacks on energy facilities. On 21 June, the cost of the war to the US military was estimated at US$40 billion, and Trump requested US$87 billion in supplementary military funding from the US Congress, with the majority of the funding request being tied to the war.
Iran, the US and Israel agreed to a two-week ceasefire brokered by Pakistan, beginning on 8 April. The Iranian ceasefire came under strain as Iran refused to re-open the Strait of Hormuz, blaming Israeli attacks on Lebanon. After the failure of the Islamabad Talks, Trump announced a naval blockade of Iran from 13 April. There remained a "dual blockade" of the Persian Gulf, by Iran and the US. On 14 June, the US and Iran announced the Islamabad Memorandum to end the war and blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. On 17 June, Trump signed the memorandum at the Palace of Versailles following the G7 summit,  and Iran's President Masoud Pezeshkian signed it in Tehran. On 18 June, the US Department of Defense removed the naval blockade of Iranian ports, but tensions escalated when Iran allegedly violated the truce with drone attacks on ships in the Strait of Hormuz. The US retaliated by striking Iranian military facilities on 26 and 27 June, leading to further Iranian missile and drone attacks on US bases. On 28 June, despite the clashes, the US and Iran agreed to cease their exchange of attacks.
