## Summary

Differential item functioning (DIF) is a statistical property of a test item that indicates how likely it is for individuals from distinct groups, possessing similar abilities, to respond differently to the item. It manifests when individuals from different groups, with comparable skill levels, do not have an equal likelihood of answering a question correctly. There are two primary types of DIF: uniform DIF, where one group consistently has an advantage over the other, and nonuniform DIF, where the advantage varies based on the individual's ability level.
The presence of DIF requires review and judgment, but it doesn't always signify bias. DIF analysis provides an indication of unexpected behavior of items on a test. DIF characteristic of an item isn't solely determined by varying probabilities of selecting a specific response among individuals from different groups. Rather, DIF becomes pronounced when individuals from different groups, who possess the same underlying true ability, exhibit differing probabilities of giving a certain response. Even when uniform bias is present, test developers sometimes resort to assumptions such as DIF biases may offset each other due to the extensive work required to address it, compromising test ethics and perpetuating systemic biases.
Common procedures for assessing DIF are Mantel-Haenszel procedure, logistic regression, item response theory (IRT) based methods, and confirmatory factor analysis (CFA) based methods.
