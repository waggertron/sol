# Source Card: Open-Vocabulary Language and Personality

## Citation

Schwartz et al. (2013). Personality, Gender, and Age in the Language of Social
Media: The Open-Vocabulary Approach. PLOS ONE.
https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0073791

Source registry id: `schwartz_2013_open_vocab_language`

## Domain

- computational linguistics
- social media language
- personality inference
- open-vocabulary methods

## Key Contribution

This paper is a major reference for using language at scale to study
personality, age, and gender. The important methodological point is that
open-vocabulary analysis can discover language clusters associated with traits
without forcing all evidence into a closed dictionary.

## Model Implications

The project should support both:

- theory-driven features, such as validated categories or questionnaire-linked
  traits
- data-driven language observations, such as recurring phrases, topics, style
  markers, and semantic clusters

For product use, language evidence should usually update communication style and
topic-interest atoms before updating stronger personality claims.

## Limits and Cautions

Associations between language and traits may be population-, platform-, culture-,
age-, and context-dependent. The system should not assume that a linguistic cue
has the same meaning across all users and settings.
