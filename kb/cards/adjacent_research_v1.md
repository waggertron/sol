# Source Cards: Adjacent Research v1

This batch imports high-standard adjacent sources around the core user-model
hypothesis. Each card is concise by design; use DOI links and follow-up imports
for deeper review.

## DeYoung 2015: Cybernetic Big Five Theory

Source registry id: `deyoung_2015_cybernetic_big_five`

Citation: DeYoung, C. G. (2015). Cybernetic Big Five Theory. Journal of
Research in Personality, 56, 33-58. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jrp.2014.07.004

Domain: stable trait tendencies; motivation; self-regulation.

Key contribution: Provides a theory linking Big Five traits to cybernetic
functions such as goal pursuit, feedback, exploration, stability, and adaptation.

Model implications: Useful for treating traits as control-system tendencies
rather than labels. Good bridge between personality traits, goals, and agent
behavior.

Limits and cautions: The biological and cybernetic mappings should be treated as
theory-informed hypotheses, not direct measurements from user data.

Evidence grade: `A`

## Roberts, Walton, and Viechtbauer 2006: Trait Change Meta-Analysis

Source registry id: `roberts_2006_personality_change_meta`

Citation: Roberts, B. W., Walton, K. E., and Viechtbauer, W. (2006). Patterns of
Mean-Level Change in Personality Traits Across the Life Course: A Meta-Analysis
of Longitudinal Studies. Psychological Bulletin, 132(1), 1-25.
https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.132.1.1

Domain: stable trait tendencies; personality development.

Key contribution: Shows that personality traits are stable enough to matter but
also change systematically across the lifespan.

Model implications: Avoid "fixed essence" language. Profile atoms need both
stability and recency fields.

Limits and cautions: Developmental population-level trends do not justify
individual predictions without longitudinal individual data.

Evidence grade: `A`

## Soto and John 2017: BFI-2 Hierarchical Facets

Source registry id: `soto_john_2017_bfi2`

Citation: Soto, C. J., and John, O. P. (2017). The Next Big Five Inventory
(BFI-2): Developing and Assessing a Hierarchical Model With 15 Facets. Journal
of Personality and Social Psychology, 113(1), 117-143.
https://doi.org/10.1037/pspp0000096

Domain: stable trait tendencies; psychometrics; facets.

Key contribution: Provides a hierarchical Big Five measurement model with
finer-grained facets.

Model implications: Supports using facets below broad traits when mapping to
generation controls.

Limits and cautions: A validated inventory does not imply that passive data can
reliably estimate each facet.

Evidence grade: `A`

## DeYoung, Quilty, and Peterson 2007: Big Five Aspects

Source registry id: `deyoung_2007_big_five_aspects`

Citation: DeYoung, C. G., Quilty, L. C., and Peterson, J. B. (2007). Between
Facets and Domains: 10 Aspects of the Big Five. Journal of Personality and
Social Psychology, 93(5), 880-896.
https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.93.5.880

Domain: stable trait tendencies; trait hierarchy.

Key contribution: Introduces aspect-level structure between broad domains and
narrow facets.

Model implications: Useful intermediate layer for profile atoms, especially when
full facet precision is not warranted.

Limits and cautions: Aspects are still trait constructs, not raw behavioral
evidence.

Evidence grade: `A`

## Fleeson 2001: Traits as Density Distributions

Source registry id: `fleeson_2001_density_distributions`

Citation: Fleeson, W. (2001). Toward a Structure- and Process-Integrated View
of Personality: Traits as Density Distributions of States. Journal of
Personality and Social Psychology, 80(6), 1011-1027.
https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.80.6.1011

Domain: context-specific states; personality dynamics.

Key contribution: Frames traits as distributions of repeated states rather than
single static points.

Model implications: Profile atoms should store variability, context, and
evidence counts, not just mean tendency.

Limits and cautions: Requires repeated observations to estimate distributions.

Evidence grade: `A`

## Cervone 2005: Personality Architecture

Source registry id: `cervone_2005_personality_architecture`

Citation: Cervone, D. (2005). Personality Architecture: Within-Person
Structures and Processes. Annual Review of Psychology, 56, 423-452.
https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev.psych.56.091103.070133

Domain: within-person modeling; context-specific states.

Key contribution: Argues for modeling within-person cognitive-affective
structures and processes instead of relying only on between-person trait scores.

Model implications: Supports a profile architecture with internal schemas,
goals, appraisals, and situation-linked patterns.

Limits and cautions: Within-person structures are difficult to infer passively
and often need direct elicitation.

Evidence grade: `A`

## Rauthmann et al. 2014: DIAMONDS Situation Taxonomy

Source registry id: `rauthmann_2014_diamonds`

Citation: Rauthmann, J. F., Sherman, R. A., Nave, C. S., and Funder, D. C.
(2014). The Situational Eight DIAMONDS: A Taxonomy of Major Dimensions of
Situation Characteristics. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 107(4),
677-718. https://doi.org/10.1037/a0037250

Domain: situation taxonomy; context-specific modes.

Key contribution: Provides a taxonomy for situation characteristics that affect
behavioral expression.

Model implications: Useful for tagging profile atoms by situation, such as duty,
adversity, intellect, sociality, or positivity contexts.

Limits and cautions: Situation labels should be inferred conservatively from
source context and user feedback.

Evidence grade: `A`

## Funder 1995: Realistic Accuracy Model

Source registry id: `funder_1995_realistic_accuracy`

Citation: Funder, D. C. (1995). On the Accuracy of Personality Judgment: A
Realistic Approach. Psychological Review, 102(4), 652-670.
https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.102.4.652

Domain: behavioral regularities; inference quality; uncertainty.

Key contribution: Explains accurate personality judgment as a chain of relevant
information availability, detection, and utilization.

Model implications: A profile atom should exist only when relevant evidence is
available, detected, and correctly interpreted.

Limits and cautions: Missing or low-quality information should produce
uncertainty, not stronger inference.

Evidence grade: `A`

## Epstein 1979: Aggregated Behavioral Stability

Source registry id: `epstein_1979_stability_behavior`

Citation: Epstein, S. (1979). The Stability of Behavior: I. On Predicting Most
of the People Much of the Time. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology,
37(7), 1097-1126. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.37.7.1097

Domain: behavioral regularities; aggregation.

Key contribution: Shows why aggregated behavior is more stable and predictive
than isolated acts.

Model implications: Single events should rarely create stable profile atoms.
Repeated evidence should increase confidence.

Limits and cautions: Aggregation can hide meaningful context-specific modes.

Evidence grade: `A`

## Pennebaker and King 1999: Linguistic Styles

Source registry id: `pennebaker_king_1999_linguistic_styles`

Psychology, 77(6), 1296-1312. https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.77.6.1296
